Why this artefact: Phase 2 design rationale for the 3-row × 5-anchor manager call-review rubric. The Phase 5 production rubric lives in 05-evaluate/l3-call-rubric.md; this file documents why that rubric looks the way it does — what was decided, by which framework lens, against which brief evidence. Inherits D-004. Frameworks: criterion-referenced rubric design (Wiggins & McTighe 2005); cognitive-load minimisation for the grader (Sweller 2011); self-determination motivational framing (Ryan & Deci 2000).

Rubric design · Phase 2 design rationale

1 · The rubric in one sentence

Three rows (M1 / M2 / M3) × five anchored levels (1–5), single A4 page, manager uses 4 calls/rep/month + week-2 + week-6 calibration sessions.

The production artefact is 05-evaluate/l3-call-rubric.md. This file is the why-not-something-else argument.

2 · Why exactly 3 rows

2.1 · Sponsor verbatim

Brief §8 K.M. (verbatim ask at week-2 follow-up):

"I want managers to walk out of the week-2 calibration session with a rubric they can use on 4 calls per rep per month. Not a 12-page document — a single sheet with 3 rows (one per move) and 1–5 scoring with anchored descriptors."

2.2 · Reference-manager prototype (highest-cadence pod)

Brief §10.6 J.T. (UK Pod 1 — fastest-ramping pod, weekly 30-min call-review cadence):

"I made my own [rubric]. Three rows — opener / objection / close — each scored 1–5 with 3 example anchors per row. Took me 6 months to develop. I'd happily share it with the program."

2.3 · Second-reference adoption commitment

Brief §10.7 M.K. (DE Pod 1 — second-highest cadence):

"Wenn ihr eine bessere baut, ich übernehme sie." ("If you build a better one, I'll adopt it.")

2.4 · The single most-requested artefact from the manager side

Brief §10.8:

"Both managers asked unprompted for a shared, calibrated 3-row rubric they could use during call reviews — and for a way to compare scores across pods so they could spot pattern misses early."

2.5 · The three rows map exactly to the three keystone moves

Brief §11 aggregate (the §9 A.S. "three things stand out from Gong reviews" — diagnostic / acknowledge / close):

  • Top decile (n = 8): M1 8/8, M2 7/7, M3 7/8 ✓
  • Bottom (n = 4): M1 0/4, M2 0/4, M3 0/4

The three moves are empirically the predictive variable in the Gong sample. Rubric rows ≠ moves would force the manager to translate between framework and observation; rubric rows = moves makes the observation immediate.

2.6 · Why not 4 rows or 8 rows

Alternative Why rejected
4 rows (add Regulatory deflection) D-004 constraint = 3 rows. v2 backlog item conditioned on Phase 5 cohort-1 Gong validation (per Module 6 Evidence note). If buyer reg questions appear in ≥ 30 % of cohort-1 calls AND deflection quality varies > 1 point across pods, add. Until then: 3 rows.
4 rows (add ICP buyer fit) ICP fit is a prerequisite to M1 + M2 + M3 — already implicitly scored in row 1 anchor descriptors. Adding a row double-scores it.
8 rows (per-component-skill C1–C9) Violates K.M.'s "not a 12-page document" constraint. Grader cognitive load would crash inter-rater reliability.
2 rows (collapse M1 + M2 into "opening", M3 standalone) Loses the M2 vs argument-reflex signal — exactly the move bottom-quartile inverts.

3 · Why exactly 5 anchored levels (1–5)

3.1 · The granularity vs grader-cognitive-load tradeoff

Per Sweller, Ayres & Kalyuga (2011): the grader's working memory is finite. Each rubric row requires the grader to hold the buyer's call audio in memory while comparing observed behaviour against anchor descriptors.

Scale Pros Cons Verdict
Pass / fail (2 levels) Lowest cognitive load Loses all coaching signal Reject — J.T. §10.6 specifically uses 1–5 for self-correction
1–3 (J.T.'s prototype) Manageable In practice, 1-2-3 collapses to "ok / not ok / bad" — middle compresses Reject — calibration variance high
1–5 levelled anchors Coaching granularity preserved; reps see exact next-level-up behaviour; calibration achievable in 1 session Slightly higher cognitive load than 1–3 Accept
1–7 Highest granularity Calibration requires multiple sessions; manager attention split too thin between calls Reject — exceeds week-6 calibration session capacity
1–10 Likert "Feels precise" but no anchors Effectively random; inter-rater near-zero Reject — anti-rubric

3.2 · The 5 anchor descriptors per row

Per l3-call-rubric.md §1:

  • 1 — Absent. The failure mode (bottom-quartile pattern from §11 Gong).
  • 2 — Partial / wrong. Attempted but mis-executed (mid-band failure mode).
  • 3 — Present but generic. Acceptable; reaches mid-band threshold.
  • 4 — Specific + situational. Top-decile entry threshold (M.G. / L.D. anchored).
  • 5 — Anchored + buyer-back-in-buyer-words. Top-decile exemplar (verbatim §10.1 / §10.2 / GC-01 / GC-06 patterns).

The 5-anchor scale is criterion-referenced (Wiggins), not norm-referenced. A rep who scores 5 on every call has reached top-decile performance; a rep scoring 3 has reached mid-band entry — independent of cohort distribution.

4 · Anchor-writing rules

Per assessment-design.md §5 + l3-call-rubric.md §6:

Rule Why Example
Every anchor at score 4 + 5 is a specific observable behaviour Eliminates "demonstrates confidence" / "shows ownership" non-anchors (Haladyna & Downing 2013 anti-pattern) M1.5 = "stays silent for ≥ 4 seconds + restates what buyer said" — not "communicates effectively"
Every score-4+5 anchor traces to a §10 SME quote or §11 Gong call Provenance discipline; an anchor that can't cite evidence is rewritten M1.5 cites M.G. §10.1 verbatim + GC-01/02/07
Failure anchors (1 + 2) trace to bottom-quartile §11 Gong patterns Asymmetry: failure modes are easier to observe than excellence M1.1 cites GC-19, GC-20 — P.B. pitch-launch at 12 s
No double-barrelled anchors Cognitive load on grader doubles when anchors conjunct two distinct behaviours Split "specific + situational" (anchor 4) from "buyer-back-in-buyer-words" (anchor 5)
One coaching focus per row, not three Lowest row is the coaching focus (per §1 totals box rule) Manager 1:1 conversation routes to weakest row, not average

5 · Calibration design — how week-2 + week-6 use the rubric

5.1 · Week 2 (post-launch · all 9 pod managers)

Per 04-implement/manager-calibration-session-plan.md (Phase 4 deliverable):

  1. Intro (5 min). Why the rubric exists (K.M. §8 ask) + framing: coaching tool, not surveillance (Ryan & Deci 2000 self-determination).
  2. Score one sample call together (15 min). A.S. picks a known top-decile call (e.g. GC-01). Managers score independently, then debate.
  3. Anchor walk-through (15 min). Each row's 5 anchors discussed against the call's actual behaviour.
  4. Pilot agreement (10 min). Managers commit to 4 calls/rep/month cadence; A.S. owns the cadence visibility dashboard.
  5. Stuck-pod carve-out (15 min). Per critic-log.md Phase 4 Pass 1 — D.W. (newly-promoted UK Pod 4 manager, no structured call review in 5 weeks per §9) gets a private 1:1 with A.S. + designer, not a group-level intervention. Honors §10.5 anti-public-shaming signal.

5.2 · Week 6 (post-launch · all 9 pod managers)

The variance-not-correctness session:

  1. Cold-score 3 calibration calls (15 min). A.S. picks 3 calls — one at score 5, one at score 3, one at score 1. Managers score cold (no debate), submit scores.
  2. Surface variance (15 min). Mini-OS / spreadsheet shows score distributions per call. Disagreements ≥ 1 point flagged.
  3. Debate (20 min). Each disagreement closed by clarifying anchor wording — not by majority vote. Updated anchors version-bump the rubric (v1.1, v1.2).
  4. Action items (10 min). Each manager commits to one anchor-clarity follow-up before next calibration.

Inter-rater reliability target. After week-6 session, ≤ 1 point variance between any two managers on a fresh sample call. Achievable per Wiggins (2005) for 5-anchor rubrics in one calibration cycle.

5.3 · Quarterly thereafter (per 06-iterate/iteration-playbook.md)

Same as week-6 cadence. Quarterly version-bumps logged in 06-iterate/rubric-version-log.md.

6 · v2 evolution path (per 06-iterate/iteration-playbook.md)

Three v2 candidates tracked. None happens in v1 — D-004 constraint holds until cohort-1 evidence justifies revision.

6.1 · 4th row · Regulatory deflection (linked to LO-REG.2)

Trigger condition. Phase 5 cohort-1 Gong validation hook (per Module 6 storyboard Evidence note):

  • Buyer reg questions appear in ≥ 30 % of cohort-1 calls, AND
  • Manager scoring of deflection quality diverges by > 1 point between pods.

If both true → add row 4 to rubric in v1.1 release. Anchors anchored to the §19 honest-carve-out vs over-deflection signal.

6.2 · Per-stakeholder columns on M1/M2 (linked to LO-PP.3)

Trigger condition. Phase 5 cohort-1 multi-stakeholder Gong validation hook (per Module 5 storyboard Evidence note):

  • Multi-stakeholder calls appear in ≥ 20 % of cohort-1 calls, AND
  • LO-PP.3 dual-diagnostic execution averages < 60 % across pods.

If both true → rubric expands to two columns per row (stakeholder-1 / stakeholder-2 scores) for M1 + M2; M3 remains single-column (multi-invitee scored once).

6.3 · ES + DE translation

Per brief §10.7 M.K. + §10.3 R.A. + §20 risk row 6. v2 deliverable.

  • Phase 6 sprint 6.1.B (translation pipeline · ES Pod first, DE Pod second).
  • Back-translation review step to validate anchor fidelity.
  • Calibration sessions in ES + DE pods continue in EN during v1; managers may discuss in local language but the rubric anchors stay in EN until v2.

7 · Why this rubric is the L3 measurement vehicle (not just a coaching tool)

Per 01-analyze/kirkpatrick-l4-l1-cascade.md §2 L3 + l3-call-rubric.md §5:

Signal Source Used for
Gong auto-tag (tag:diagnostic-opener etc.) NLP, free, nightly, high false-positive on M2 Aggregate trend lines
Manager rubric score Human judgement against anchors, ~ 5 min/call × 4 calls/rep/month, low noise after calibration Coaching + L3 dashboard scoring

The two signals must not be conflated. Auto-tag drives the L3 adherence metric (brief §5 "talk-track adherence weeks 4-8 → ≥ 80 %"); the rubric drives the quality metric (§5 "objection-handling rubric → ≥ 3.5 / 5"). Cross-check rule per l3-call-rubric.md §5: if auto-tag adherence and rubric score diverge by > 1.5 points on the same call sample, recalibrate. Tag drift = first hypothesis.

8 · Self-determination framing (per Ryan & Deci 2000)

The rubric is positioned for the rep as a self-coaching tool, not surveillance. Per frameworks-applied.md §18:

  • Telemetry stays private (rep + manager + K.M.); no leaderboard per §10.5 exit-interview signal.
  • Coaching focus = lowest row, not the average (autonomy support — the rep chooses one move to improve, not the sum).
  • Anchors are visible to reps before manager review (J.T. §10.6: "reps know what I'm grading them on so they self-correct between calls" — competence support).
  • Calibration is peer-to-peer between managers (relatedness support).

The rubric design honors all three SDT pillars without making them a separate intervention.
