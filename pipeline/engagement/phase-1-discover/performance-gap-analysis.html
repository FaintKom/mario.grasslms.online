02

Why the top decile is hard to copy

curse-of-expertise check

The top reps "sound nothing alike" (A.S., week 1) and cannot articulate what they do differently — when asked, they say things like "I just listen" or "I read the room." This is the standard curse-of-expertise failure mode: experts have internalised their move-set so deeply they cannot teach it to novices by demonstration alone.

Practical consequence for the program: direct mentoring by top reps will not work as the primary intervention. The intervention has to extract the three observable moves into a teachable framework, then drill those moves under low-stakes simulated conditions until they are loaded into recall. This is the 4C/ID whole-task case par excellence.

Evidence: in week 2 interviews, when asked "what do you do that newer reps don't?" both top-decile reps initially gave abstract answers ("I'm just curious about the buyer"). When shown specific call snippets, both could name the three moves — but neither had previously articulated them as a system. The framework is more valuable than the mentor.

03

Time-to-skill-acquisition · current vs. target

why ramp time is a behaviour problem, not a knowledge problem

Salesforce ramp-time data (last 4 cohorts, 48 reps) sliced by tenure-band:

Cohort week % of reps consistently showing all 3 moves Inferred cause
Week 4 (post-current-onboarding) ~ 18 % Current onboarding teaches product knowledge + tool fluency. The three behavioural moves are not explicitly taught.
Week 8 ~ 34 % Reps who survive get the moves by manager-1:1 osmosis — uneven across pods, hence variance.
Week 12 ~ 51 % By month 3, surviving reps have absorbed the moves from call-shadowing top performers. ~ 17 % churn before this point.
Top decile (mature) ~ 88 % Internalised. Articulation gap remains (see §02).

Implication: current onboarding leaves the three keystone moves to chance — picked up by osmosis, with manager-cadence variance as the primary determinant of whether a rep gets them by week 8 or not at all. Compressing ramp by 25 % means moving the "consistently shows all 3 moves" milestone from week 12 to week 8, and the week-4 floor from 18 % to ≥ 60 %.

04

Mager root-cause matrix · what training can and cannot fix

honest scope cap — three causes, three verdicts

Mager's framing (Mager & Pipe, Analyzing Performance Problems, 1970/1997): if a perfectly trained employee were dropped into this role tomorrow, would they hit the target? The non-training root causes named below were surfaced at kickoff by K.M. and A.S., re-confirmed in 4 of 10 SME interviews. K.M. accepted the cap in writing — see Needs Assessment §03.

Cause A · Manager 1:1 cadence variance

Evidence: 2 of 4 UK pods run weekly 30-min call reviews with rubrics; 1 of 4 has not had a call review in 5 weeks. DE pods marginally better but inconsistent.

Effect on ramp: reps in low-cadence pods take ~ 30 % longer to hit week-8 milestone. Direct correlation with bottom-quartile churn (3 of last 4 exit-interviews cited "no feedback on my calls" as a top-3 reason for leaving).

IN SCOPE · partial

Training cannot fix manager behaviour directly; the program will put a floor under it via a shared 1:1 rubric + week-2 and week-6 calibration sessions for managers. Cap: we lift the floor, we do not redesign manager hiring or 1:1 culture.

Cause B · Marketing lead-quality decline

Evidence: inbound close-rate dropped 12 % → 8 % over last two quarters (K.M., kickoff). Three SDR interviews cited "leads have gotten noticeably worse" unprompted.

Effect on ramp: a rep with the three moves loaded still cannot close stale or mis-fit leads. Estimated to account for ~ 20–30 % of bottom-quartile churn (cannot be isolated cleanly without an A/B; quoted as range).

OUT OF SCOPE

K.M. owns the CMO conversation. Documented in Needs Assessment §08. The 25 % ramp-compression target assumes lead quality stabilises; the engagement cannot guarantee the target if lead quality continues to decline.

Cause C · Comp plan SPIF distortion

Evidence: SPIFs reward closed-won regardless of pipeline source. New reps grind on stale leads chasing the SPIF instead of working the fresh day-1 list. Documented in 2 of 10 SME interviews + A.S. observation.

Effect on ramp: new reps spend ~ 30 % of week-3-to-8 dialling on opportunities outside their assigned territory. Lowers the denominator on calls-with-ICP-fit, slows ramp.

OUT OF SCOPE

RevOps-owned redesign. Surfaced to J.V. for handling separately. Documented in Needs Assessment §08.

Net: two of three named non-training causes are out of scope; one is partially addressable. K.M. signed the cap. This is the honest framing the engagement is operating under.

05

What this means for the training intervention

the recommendations the program is designed to execute
REC 1
Frame the program around the three keystone moves (M1 / M2 / M3), not around product or tool knowledge. Bottom-quartile reps do not have a product-knowledge gap — they have a behavioural-recall gap. Existing onboarding already covers product + tooling; this engagement targets the call-motion craft layer specifically.
REC 2
Use 4C/ID whole-task simulation as the primary instructional model. The keystone moves are recall-under-pressure skills, not declarative knowledge. Whole-task simulated call practice with branching scenarios is the only mode of practice that loads the moves into Sam's call-time recall. (See Phase 3 · 4C/ID architecture for the spec.)
REC 3
Build a manager-side reinforcement layer. The training-side floor on manager 1:1 quality is achievable via a shared call-review rubric + two calibration sessions (week 2 + week 6). Anything beyond that is a separate engagement.
REC 4
Use Gong + Salesforce as the L3 measurement instruments. The three moves are all observable on existing Gong recordings (opener-tag, objection-handling rubric, next-step calendar field). No new instrumentation required.
REC 5
Document the lead-quality + comp-plan dependencies in the sponsor sign-off. If the engagement's L4 target is missed at six months and the non-training causes have not been resolved on K.M.'s and J.V.'s side, the attribution conversation has to be honest. The phase-1 summary contains the language to defend the cap.
06

What I would change next iteration of this analysis

honest limitations
  • →
    Bigger Gong sample. 22 calls is sufficient to surface the three moves but not to estimate prevalence with tight confidence intervals. Phase 6 measurement plan includes a 100-call sample per cohort to refine the baseline.
  • →
    Quantify the lead-quality contribution. The 20–30 % bottom-quartile-churn attribution range is a sponsor-side estimate; a controlled comparison (cohort with current lead mix vs. cohort with curated leads) would isolate the training-side contribution. Out of engagement scope but recommended to RevOps.
  • →
    Manager-side interviews more depth. 2 manager interviews is the minimum viable; a future iteration should include 5–6 to map cadence variance to behavioural outcomes more rigorously.
