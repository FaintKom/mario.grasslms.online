02 · The problem

Two KPIs on one dashboard, and a sponsor allergic to slide-decks

K.M., VP Sales Enablement, opened kickoff with two paragraphs that framed the entire engagement. They are reproduced verbatim because every design choice downstream answers to them.

“Ramp time for new SDRs is sitting at ~95 days to first paid quota attainment. Top decile hits it in ~52. Bottom quartile churns out before day 120 without ever paying for themselves. We need to compress mean ramp by 25 % and stop the bottom-quartile churn — losing two reps before they pay back costs us ~€90K each in unrecovered onboarding.”

“I’ve committed −25 % ramp compression to the CRO. That’s the public number. If we hit it, we hit it together. If we miss it, I need to know whether the cause was training or lead quality or comp, because the conversation with the CRO is honest either way.”

— K.M., VP Sales Enablement · brief §4 + §8

The audit verdict from Phase 1: the existing 6-week program teaches product and tools correctly, but does not teach the three keystone behavioural moves explicitly. Reps either pick them up by osmosis from top performers (slow, uneven) or never pick them up (churn). The bottom quartile loses signal after week 4, when manager 1:1 cadence variance kicks in.

Baseline: 95 d mean ramp · 52 d top decile · >120 d bottom quartile · 83 % day-120 retention · ~17 % bottom-quartile churn pre-payback · ~€90K unrecovered onboarding per lost rep.

04 · The keystone moves

Three moves, surfaced from 22 Gong calls

“Three things stand out from Gong reviews. One — top reps spend the first 60 seconds of every cold call asking one diagnostic question that exposes which pain to pitch against. The rest of them launch into the pitch in the first 15 seconds. Two — when the buyer pushes back, top reps re-state the objection back in the buyer’s own words before responding. The rest of them argue. Three — top reps end every call with a written next step booked in the calendar before they hang up. The rest say ‘I’ll follow up.’”

— A.S., Head of SDR · brief §9
M1

Diagnostic Opener

One observation + one question in the first 60 seconds. Then a five-second silence — “feels like an hour but it’s where the deal happens.”

Top decile 8 / 8 · Bottom 0 / 4
M2

Objection Acknowledge

Re-state the objection in the buyer’s own words before responding. ≥ 2 token overlap with what the buyer said. Then wait.

Top decile 7 / 7 · Bottom 0 / 4
M3

Calendar Close

Calendar open in a second tab. Two slots read aloud, invite sent during the call. Never “I’ll follow up.”

Top decile 7 / 8 · Bottom 0 / 4

Average call duration · top decile 8:42 · mid 4:11 · bottom 0:31. Source: brief §11 Gong aggregate (n=22).

08 · Critic loop

Agentic self-critique — each phase walked through a cold reader

Every phase artefact bundle was reviewed by a separate critic agent before sign-off. The critic ran as a fresh subagent with no access to the drafting context — it saw only the brief, the artefacts, and the phase-gate criteria. Its job was to ask whether the deliverables held up to a cold reader, verify every quantitative claim back to the brief or a decision-log entry, and surface the top three issues a sponsor like K.M. would raise on first read.

Up to three critic passes per phase; the phase only advanced after a PASS verdict. Pass 1 for Phase 4 surfaced three date-arithmetic and citation-hygiene issues; the follow-up rework closed all three and the verdict was re-affirmed. Pass 1 for Phase 6 caught a slide-4 cohort-count contradiction with brief §21 and a sandbagging-flirt closing line on slide 5; both rewritten in a single revision pass. Phase 3 (Develop) returned a CONDITIONAL PASS — three fixes (M2 manifest under-declaration, event-log spec drift, SCORM Cloud preview log) — all closed in one rework. The full audit trail is in critic-log.md.

The critic loop is the structural answer to the kickoff instruction: work iteratively like in agile, but also run yourself as a critic to fix what you produced. Phase-gates are the agile sprint review; the critic agent is the cold-reader stand-in for K.M.
